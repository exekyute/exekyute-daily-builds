Every monster-collector I love has the same problem: the best community guide is a wall of
text on a dated wiki, wrapped in autoplay video and an anti-adblock nag. The data is great.
The delivery is from another decade.

## The data is structured; the guides are not

A creature in one of these games *is* structured data - a type, a stat line, an evolution
path, a where-to-find. That is a database row. So why does every guide flatten it into prose
and a stacked column of screenshots?

<Callout type="tip" title="The fix is boring and obvious">
Treat each creature like a record: an infobox for the stats, a sortable table for the
movepool, a real gallery for the art, and a checkbox so the reader can track what they have
caught. None of that is hard. It just is not what BBCode or a 2010 wiki theme lets you do.
</Callout>

## What a modern monster guide should do

- **Let you check things off.** Catching is the loop; the guide should track the loop.
- **Sort and filter.** "Show me everything weak to Fire under level 20" is one click, not a Ctrl+F prayer.
- **Show the art properly.** Flick through sprites in a lightbox instead of scrolling past them.
- **Load instantly.** These are reference pages you open mid-battle. Twelve megabytes of ads is hostile.

## That is the whole pitch

This site is me building the guide I wish existed for the games I actually play. The
[Whistlewood walkthrough](/guides/whistlewood-100-walkthrough/) is the first proper
test of it - checklists, a sortable duet table, and a gallery, all on a page that loads
before you can blink.

More to come. The genre deserves it.
